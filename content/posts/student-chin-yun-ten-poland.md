In March 2025, I flew all the way to Gdańsk, Poland to do my Undergraduate Research II. Upon arrival, I was greeted by Prof. Tomasz Paterek's warm welcome and his statement of "It's a warm day today!" — when it was like 9°C outside.

## The Research

I work with Prof. Thomas Zlosnik (Prof. Tom) in the Institute of Theoretical Physics and Astrophysics of the University of Gdańsk. We are working on the **"Cosmological Consequences of an Extension to General Relativity"**. Prof. Tom and fellow researchers had proposed a gravitational Model B, an extension to Einstein's theory of General Relativity, where gravity has "no degrees of freedom". We investigate the probability that our universe can be described by Model B in its early phase, and eventually underwent inflation to arrive at its current state. To develop a path to gravitation, we used parametrized field theory to construct an action, and then analysed the equations of motion. We obtained new solutions and compared them to General Relativity for insights and further studies.

## Day-to-Day Life at the Institute

I have a lot of freedom here to work at my own pace. Almost every weekday, I go to Office 366 — we call it the **"PhD incubator"** — to do my work. To discuss research progress, we arrange meetings with Prof. Tom. We always have a pleasant time talking, whether it's about physics or life.

To give a spark of glitter and magic to the working environment, I started drawing bunnies on the whiteboard. Surprisingly, it drew people together — everyone was having so much fun with the bunnies! Friends would pay attention to any new bunny, and add their own remarks. It even brought in people from other faculties to leave their bunny on the board. This was definitely one of the most wholesome things to happen during my research period.

## Life in Poland

To me, aside from the amazing research environment, Poland is genuinely a very nice place to live in. I mostly walk — they have nice pedestrian streets, and it always feels so refreshing to have a nice walk to the office before working, or after meals, or to anywhere. I've picked up the habit of walking when the destination isn't too far away. I also take public transport quite often.

Aside from Gdańsk where my research is based, I visited other cities as well, including Sopot, Gdynia, Toruń, Warsaw, Kraków and Wrocław. I went to old towns, museums, parks, forests, mines, castles, towers, riverbanks, beaches, and grocery stores (this is very important). Among the places I've visited, my favourite is undoubtedly the Słowiński National Park — I had never felt so close to nature, sinking my feet into the fine, grain-like sand of the moving dunes.

## Reflections

The experience has been profoundly enriching — not just academically, but personally. Research abroad opens your mind to different ways of thinking, different cultures, and different approaches to physics. I am deeply grateful to Prof. Tomasz Paterek for making this opportunity possible, and to Prof. Tom for his patient and inspiring mentorship throughout the internship.
